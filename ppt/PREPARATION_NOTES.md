# SECTION 1: UNDERSTANDING THE BASICS (Read this first)

## 1.1 What is this project about?

Sunder Nursery is a beautiful 90-acre park in Delhi with old Mughal monuments, gardens, birds, and wetlands. The entry ticket costs Rs. 20-30. But is the park really worth only Rs. 20-30? Obviously not - people travel long distances, spend hours there, and deeply care about its preservation. The park provides benefits that have no price tag: peace of mind, beauty, heritage, clean air, bird habitat, and so on.

**Our job:** Put a number on the true economic value of Sunder Nursery - how much is it actually worth to people?

**The problem:** You can't just look at the ticket price because that's artificially low. And there's no "market" where people buy and sell "enjoyment of Sunder Nursery." So how do you measure the value of something that isn't bought or sold?

**The answer:** Economists have developed special methods for this. We used two of them.

---

## 1.2 The Two Methods We Used

### Method 1: CVM (Contingent Valuation Method) - OUR MAIN METHOD

**Plain English:** You directly ask people: "How much would you pay?"

**Analogy:** Imagine a park is about to be closed unless visitors pay more. You ask visitors: "Would you be willing to pay Rs. 50 instead of Rs. 30 to keep this park open?" Their answers tell you how much the park is worth to them.

**Why it's called "contingent":** Because the payment is contingent (dependent) on a hypothetical scenario - "IF the park needed more money for conservation, WOULD you pay more?"

**What makes it special:** It's the ONLY method that can capture ALL types of value - including the value people place on the park even if they never visit it (more on this below).

**How we used it:** We asked 45 visitors at Sunder Nursery: "Would you pay a slightly higher entry fee to guarantee permanent protection of the park's monuments, gardens, and birds?" If yes, how much extra - Rs. 10, 20, 50, or 100?

### Method 2: TCM (Travel Cost Method) - OUR SUPPORTING METHOD

**Plain English:** Instead of asking people what they'd pay, you look at what they already spend just to get there.

**Analogy:** If someone drives 30 km, spends Rs. 200 on petrol/auto, and gives up 2 hours of their time just to visit a park with a Rs. 30 ticket - clearly, the park is worth way more than Rs. 30 to them. Their travel cost reveals their true valuation.

**Why it's called "revealed preference":** Because you're not asking people hypothetical questions - you're observing their actual behavior (how much they actually spent to come).

**Limitation in our case:** 53% of our respondents said they were also visiting nearby places (like Humayun's Tomb next door). So their travel cost is shared between multiple destinations. We can't say "they spent Rs. 200 just for Sunder Nursery" when they were also going to Humayun's Tomb. That's why we used TCM only as a supporting estimate, not the main one.

---

## 1.3 Types of Value - Why a Park is Worth More Than You Think

Think about why Sunder Nursery matters. There are different layers:

### USE VALUE (you personally use the park)
- You walk through the gardens, enjoy the flowers, watch birds, sit by the lake
- This is the direct benefit you get from physically being there
- TCM measures this - your travel cost reveals how much this experience is worth to you

### NON-USE VALUE (you care about the park even beyond your own visits)
Non-use value has three parts:

**a) Option Value:**
"I might want to visit next year, so I want the park to stay open."
Like buying insurance - you pay to keep the option available.

**b) Bequest Value:**
"I want my children and grandchildren to be able to enjoy this park."
The satisfaction of knowing future generations will benefit.

**c) Existence Value:**
"Even if I move to another city and never come back, I'm happy knowing this park exists and is protected."
You value its mere existence, independent of any personal use.

### TOTAL ECONOMIC VALUE (TEV)
```
TEV = Use Value + Non-Use Value
    = Use Value + (Option Value + Bequest Value + Existence Value)
```

**Why this matters:** If the government only considers the ticket revenue (Rs. 20 x visitors), they massively undervalue the park. A government might think "this land is worth more as a shopping mall." TEV shows that the park's true value - including the fact that millions of people want it to exist - is enormous.

---

## 1.4 What is "Willingness to Pay" (WTP)?

WTP is the maximum amount of money a person would be willing to give up for something.

**Example:** If you'd pay up to Rs. 50 extra to keep Sunder Nursery protected, your WTP is Rs. 50. If your friend would pay Rs. 20, their WTP is Rs. 20. If your uncle says "I wouldn't pay anything extra," his WTP is Rs. 0.

**Mean WTP:** The average WTP across all respondents. This is the single number we use to represent what the "typical visitor" would pay.

**Why we care:** If we know the average WTP and the number of visitors, we can estimate the total value of the park to society.

---

## 1.5 What is "Consumer Surplus"?

**Plain English:** The bonus value you get because something costs less than what you'd actually pay for it.

**Analogy:** You're dying of thirst and would pay Rs. 100 for a bottle of water. The shop sells it for Rs. 20. You pay Rs. 20 but you valued it at Rs. 100. Your consumer surplus is Rs. 100 - Rs. 20 = Rs. 80. You got Rs. 80 worth of "free bonus happiness."

**For Sunder Nursery:** Someone who spends Rs. 200 traveling to a park with a Rs. 30 entry fee clearly values the experience at Rs. 200+. Their consumer surplus is the gap between what they'd pay and what they actually paid.

---

## 1.6 What is a "Protest Bid"?

When you ask "would you pay extra?", some people say No - but not because the park is worthless to them. They say No because they think:
- "The GOVERNMENT should pay for conservation, not me as a visitor"
- This is a protest against the payment mechanism, not against the park's value

These are called **protest bids**. In proper CVM analysis, you identify them and optionally exclude them, because including them unfairly drags down the WTP estimate.

In our survey, 5 out of 15 "No" respondents said "government should pay" - these are the protest bids. If we remove them, the WTP rate goes from 66.7% to 75%.

```
Median WTP (the middle value when sorted):

= Rs. 26.67

```

```
If each visitor is willing to pay Rs. 26.7 extra on average,
and the park gets roughly 15,00,000 (15 lakh) visitors per year:

Aggregate Annual WTP = Rs. 26.7 x 15,00,000
                     = Rs. 4,00,05,000
                     = approximately Rs. 4.00 crore per year
```

**What this means:** Indian visitors collectively value the conservation of Sunder Nursery at about Rs. 4 crore per year, based on what they say they'd be willing to pay.

## 2.8 THE SECOND BIG CALCULATION: Non-Use Multiplier

We need to convert those percentage responses into a monetary estimate. Here's how:

### The fractions:
- Bequest fraction = 31/45 = 0.689 (i.e., 68.9% said "very important")
- Existence fraction = 37/45 = 0.822 (i.e., 82.2% said "yes")

### The weights:
We assign conservative weights to convert these sentiments into monetary scaling factors:
- Bequest weight = 0.5
- Existence weight = 0.3

**Why these weights?** Saying "preservation is important" doesn't mean you'd double your payment. Not every sentiment translates fully into money. The weights are conservative scaling factors. Bequest gets a higher weight (0.5) because it involves a more active commitment (preserving for future generations). Existence gets a lower weight (0.3) because it's a more passive sentiment (just knowing it exists).

**For comparison:** In the Lumpinee Park study from our ebook, accounting for existence value alone caused the park's value to jump from $6.6 million to $58 million - nearly a 9x multiplier. Our multiplier of 1.59x is far more conservative.

### The calculation:
```
Total multiplier = 1 (base) + (0.689 x 0.5) + (0.822 x 0.3)
                 = 1 + 0.3445 + 0.2466
                 = 1.59

This means: when you include non-use values, the total CVM value is
1.59 times the direct WTP.

Non-use component = (1.59 - 1) x Rs. 4.00 crore
                  = 0.59 x Rs. 4.00 crore
                  = Rs. 2.36 crore per year
```

---

## 2.9 THE THIRD BIG CALCULATION: Travel Cost Method (Consumer Surplus)

### Step 1: Convert travel cost categories to midpoint values

Since respondents chose from ranges (not exact amounts), we use the midpoint of each range:

| Category | Midpoint | Number of people |
|----------|----------|-----------------|
| Rs. 0-20 | Rs. 10 | 4 |
| Rs. 21-50 | Rs. 35 | 8 |
| Rs. 51-100 | Rs. 75 | 15 |
| Rs. 101-200 | Rs. 150 | 11 |
| More than Rs. 200 | Rs. 250 | 7 |

### Step 2: Calculate mean travel cost
```
Total travel cost = (4 x 10) + (8 x 35) + (15 x 75) + (11 x 150) + (7 x 250)
                  = 40 + 280 + 1125 + 1650 + 1750
                  = Rs. 4,845

Mean one-way travel cost = 4,845 / 45 = Rs. 107.7
Mean round-trip = Rs. 215.3
```

### Step 3: Calculate consumer surplus

The idea: the maximum observed travel cost (Rs. 250, the midpoint of "more than Rs. 200") represents the "ceiling" - the most anyone was willing to spend to come here. Each person's consumer surplus is the difference between this ceiling and their actual cost.

```
Person in Rs. 0-20 group: CS = 250 - 10 = Rs. 240 each
Person in Rs. 21-50 group: CS = 250 - 35 = Rs. 215 each
Person in Rs. 51-100 group: CS = 250 - 75 = Rs. 175 each
Person in Rs. 101-200 group: CS = 250 - 150 = Rs. 100 each
Person in Rs. 200+ group: CS = 250 - 250 = Rs. 0 each (they're at the max)

Total sample CS = (4 x 240) + (8 x 215) + (15 x 175) + (11 x 100) + (7 x 0)
                = 960 + 1720 + 2625 + 1100 + 0
                = Rs. 6,405

CS per visitor = 6,405 / 45 = Rs. 142.3
```

### Step 4: Scale to annual level
```
Annual Consumer Surplus = Rs. 142.3 x 15,00,000 visitors
                        = Rs. 21,34,50,000
                        = Rs. 21.35 crore per year
```

# SECTION 4: EVERY QUESTION THE PROF COULD ASK

## Basics

**Q: "What is CVM in one line?"**
> "CVM is a survey-based technique where you create a hypothetical market and directly ask people their willingness to pay for a non-market environmental good."

**Q: "What is TCM in one line?"**
> "TCM is a technique that infers the value of a recreational site from the actual travel costs visitors incur to reach it."

**Q: "What is the difference between stated and revealed preference?"**
> "Stated preference (CVM) = you ASK people what they'd hypothetically pay. Revealed preference (TCM) = you OBSERVE what they actually spend. Both have pros and cons. CVM can capture non-use values but may have hypothetical bias. TCM is based on real behavior but misses non-use values."

**Q: "What is TEV?"**
> "Total Economic Value is the sum of use value (direct enjoyment from visiting) and non-use value (option, bequest, and existence values). It captures the complete worth of an environmental resource to society."

**Q: "What are use and non-use values?"**
> "Use value is what you get from actually using the park. Non-use value is what you get from knowing the park exists and is preserved, independent of your own visits. Non-use value has three parts: option value (keeping the option to visit later), bequest value (preserving for future generations), and existence value (satisfaction from knowing it exists)."

**Q: "What is consumer surplus?"**
> "It's the difference between what you'd be willing to pay for something and what you actually pay. If you'd pay Rs. 200 for a park visit but the ticket is Rs. 30, your consumer surplus is Rs. 170 - that's the 'bonus value' you enjoy for free."

**Q: "What is a payment vehicle?"**
> "It's the mechanism through which the hypothetical payment would be collected. We used a higher entry fee because visitors already pay one, so it feels realistic. Alternatives could be taxes or donations, but those feel more hypothetical."

**Q: "What is a protest bid?"**
> "When a CVM respondent says 'No, I won't pay' not because they think the resource is worthless, but because they object to who should pay or how. Example: 'The government should pay, not visitors.' The person values the park but protests the funding approach."

## About Our Specific Study

**Q: "Why CVM over TCM as primary method?"**
> "Two reasons. First, CVM can capture non-use values (bequest, existence) which TCM cannot - the ebook explicitly states this as a drawback of TCM. Second, 53% of our respondents were on multi-destination trips, which makes TCM unreliable since travel costs are shared."

**Q: "Why these specific bid amounts (Rs. 10, 20, 50, 100)?"**
> "The current entry fee is Rs. 20-30. Our bids range from a very modest Rs. 10 extra to Rs. 100 extra (roughly tripling the fee). This gives a realistic range without going into territory that would seem absurd. The ebook recommends closed-ended choices because people are unfamiliar with pricing environmental goods."

**Q: "Where did 15 lakh annual visitors come from?"**
> "This is the estimated annual footfall for Sunder Nursery based on publicly available data. At roughly 4,000-5,000 visitors per day, 15 lakh per year is a reasonable estimate for a popular urban park in Delhi."

**Q: "Why did students show higher WTP than high-income groups?"**
> "Three reasons: (1) The extra amounts are small (Rs. 10-20), so income isn't a real barrier for anyone. (2) Students tend to have stronger environmental awareness and idealism. (3) Many students are frequent visitors with emotional attachment. This pattern is actually consistent with CVM literature."

**Q: "Where did the weights 0.5 and 0.3 come from for the non-use multiplier?"**
> "These are conservative scaling factors we chose. There's no standard formula in the literature for converting survey percentages into monetary multipliers. We chose 0.5 for bequest (stronger, more active commitment) and 0.3 for existence (more passive sentiment). For context, in the Lumpinee Park study from our ebook, existence value alone caused a 9x multiplier. Our 1.59x is far more conservative."

**Q: "Why not use median WTP instead of mean?"**
> "The median among willing respondents is Rs. 20, which is lower than the mean of Rs. 40. We used the mean because it captures the full distribution including high-WTP respondents. But we report both. Using median would give a more conservative estimate of about Rs. 2 crore annually instead of Rs. 4 crore."

**Q: "Is Rs. 142 consumer surplus per visitor realistic?"**
> "Yes. This represents the total travel expenditure surplus, not what they'd pay extra for a ticket. Someone who spent Rs. 200 on an auto to visit a park with a Rs. 30 ticket has revealed that the visit is worth at least Rs. 200 to them. The Rs. 142 average is the surplus above the cheapest observed travel cost, which is reasonable for visitors coming from across Delhi."

**Q: "What biases might affect your results?"**
> "Four main biases from CVM literature: (1) Strategic bias - people may understate WTP to free-ride or overstate it to look environmentally conscious. (2) Hypothetical bias - no real money was exchanged. (3) Information bias - we couldn't fully explain conservation costs in a 2-minute survey. (4) Sample bias - we surveyed only on one Friday evening."

**Q: "Is 45 respondents enough?"**
> "For a classroom pilot study, yes - it's enough to demonstrate the methodology and identify clear patterns. For actual policy recommendations, no - you'd need 200+. The Lumpinee Park study in the ebook used 187 respondents."

**Q: "What if someone asks about Hedonic Pricing or Replacement Cost?"**
> Hedonic Pricing: Not applicable here. It works where environmental attributes are reflected in property values (e.g., houses near a landfill are cheaper). A park's value to visitors isn't captured in housing prices of nearby areas in any straightforward way.
> Replacement Cost: Theoretically possible (what would it cost to recreate a 90-acre heritage park from scratch?) but gives an astronomical number and doesn't capture the subjective value people place on this specific park.

**Q: "What policy would you recommend?"**
> "(1) A modest fee increase of Rs. 20-50 is acceptable to most visitors and could generate Rs. 3-7.5 crore annually for conservation. (2) The 'government should pay' sentiment supports continued public funding alongside user fees. (3) Programs like bird walks and heritage tours could engage the high-WTP young demographic."

## Deep Conceptual Questions

**Q: "How is CVM different from just asking 'how much do you like this park?'"**
> "CVM doesn't just ask opinions - it asks for a monetary commitment. It creates a realistic scenario: 'the park needs more funding; would you pay Rs. X more?' This forces people to think about real trade-offs with their money, not just express vague preferences. The payment vehicle (entry fee) and the specific amounts (Rs. 10/20/50/100) make it concrete."

**Q: "The ebook mentions the Lumpinee Park study. How does our study compare?"**
> "Lumpinee Park is a public park in Bangkok with a nominal entry fee. Dixon and Hufschmidt (1986) surveyed 187 visitors from 17 districts using TCM. They found consumer surplus of $660,230 annually. When they accounted for existence value, the park's capitalized value jumped from $6.6 million to $58 million - a 9x increase. Our study is similar in approach but smaller in scale (45 vs 187 respondents), and our non-use multiplier (1.59x) is much more conservative than their 9x."

**Q: "The ebook mentions the Colorado Wilderness study. How is it relevant?"**
> "Walsh et al. (1984) surveyed 218 Colorado households about wilderness preservation. They found that non-use (preservation) values represented 37% of total value even at the lower end. This proved that non-use values are real and significant. In our study, the CVM non-use component (Rs. 2.36 crore) is about 37% of total CVM value (Rs. 6.36 crore) - remarkably consistent with their finding."

**Q: "What is the Kalamazoo Nature Center and why is it relevant?"**
> "It's a CVM case study from the ebook. Their survey design is similar to ours: they asked what aspects visitors value, whether they'd pay more in taxes, how much, and probed bequest and existence values. Our questionnaire closely mirrors their approach, adapted for an Indian urban park context."

**Q: "Can you explain the demand curve in the TCM analysis?"**
> "The demand curve shows the relationship between travel cost and the number of visitors. As travel cost increases, fewer people visit - this is the basic law of demand. We plotted the percentage of visitors at each travel cost level. The area under this curve (above the entry fee) is the consumer surplus. It's essentially saying: the demand for the park is strong because people are willing to spend a lot just to get here."
