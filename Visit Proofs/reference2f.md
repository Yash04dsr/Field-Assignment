##### Hedonic valuation in practice:

- Estimate overall price change due to a given
    environmental attribute or amenity statistically.
- Establish functional relationship between a set of
    independent and dependent variables.
- In the case of housing, the independent variables
    include location, lot size, scenery, number of
    rooms, floor space, mechanical systems, age, and
    property tax level, distance from office, school etc.
- The dependent variables are observed market
    prices


- Data fitted statistically to produce estimated

##### coefficients for an equation called the hedonic

##### price function.

- The coefficients of the price function express a

##### marginal price associated with each attribute.

- Example- estimate the price effects of landfill

##### sites on house values.

- On a percentage basis, house value rises by

##### about 6. 2 % per km from the landfill’.


- Problem- hedonic price function remain

##### constant regardless of the level of the

##### environmental quality;

- In above example unit value be higher for

##### homes closer to the landfill site.

- Economists approach this problem by applying

##### a statistical technique known as a second stage

##### of the hedonic valuation methods - two-stage

##### hedonic valuation method

- One-unit change and a multi-unit change from the
    sample average value


- The adverse effect on home values 12 % for

##### homes located at the landfill boundary and 6

##### % at about one km and negligible for homes

##### located beyond two km from the landfill.

- Hedonic price approach based on people’s

##### actual behavior has great utility.

- Represents a large share of most consumers’

##### income (welfare).

- Value attached to the residential environment

##### represent a large share of the overall value

##### attached to environmental quality.

    morbidity (illness) associated with occupational
    choices.
- Willingness to pay inferred from available data on

- Pollution an environmental factor exposes humans
    to some degree of health risk.

#### How to Monetize Mortality or Morbidity

- How to measure environmental effect increasing
    mortality and morbidity rates of a community in
    monetary terms?
- Measurement of human ‘life’, pain and suffering?
- Life is priceless?
- No measurement of values of ‘life’ or ‘pain’ but
    people’s preferences for health risk – how much
    damage people willing to avoid.


- Risk of various degree in all activity
- ‘life’?? - a ‘statistical life’.
- When applying the hedonic price approach,
    morbidity risks associated with workplace
    environmental hazards assumed to be factored
    into wages paid by different occupations.
- Wage–risk differentials used for measuring
    changes in morbidity resulting from
    environmental pollution.


- Average wage rate of coal miners much higher
    than blue-collar workers in the manufacturing
    sector.
- Compensating wage differential, sufficient to
    entice a worker to accept a less desirable or a
    more hazardous job.
- Problem: (i) assumes that workers have economic
    freedom to choose among alternative
    occupations. (ii) how well a compensating wage
    differential can be used as a measure of
    ‘preferences’ for reducing mortality risk.


- Statistical methods used to construct a functional
    relationship between the level of workers’
    compensation and environmental risk.
- Dependent variables - wage rates for similar
    occupational categories;
- Independent variables - attributes of risk, such as
    different exposure levels to environmental
    hazards.
- Fitting of data into hedonic wage function
- Major challenge to isolate the difference in wage
    associated with difference in safety.


- Statistically fitted hedonic wage function reveal
    how much compensation, on average, workers
    require in order to accept more environmental
    risk.
- Another area where hedonic valuation considered
    is in the economic evaluation of premature
    mortality.
- Economic value of interest approximated by
    society’s loss of labor productivity as a result of an
    individual’s premature death caused by specific
    pollution-related ailments
- Calculation of the % discounted value of future
    earnings that lost due to premature mortality.

#### 4 The household production function

#### approach

A. Aversive expenditures

- households’ expenditures on goods and/or services
    that are substitutes or complements for the purpose
    of avoiding environmental damage.
- Examples of such types of household expenditure
    include installing soundproof walling; purchasing
    water filters; frequent hospital visits; frequent
    painting of residential dwellings; and so on.


- households are willing to pay a certain amount of
    money (price) to avert specific environmental
    damage(s).
- So commonly known as aversive expenditures-
    measure of households’ willingness to oneself from
    exposure to environmental damage.
- in many cases, in order to attain a given change in
    environmental quality, several types of aversive
    expenditures may be undertaken simultaneously.
- In this situation, total benefit is measured by
    summing the various expenditures


B Travel cost method

- Another variation of the household production
    function approach involves the valuation of
    environmental services from recreational sites, such
    as national parks.
- A special technique that is used to estimate the
    benefit from changes in the environmental amenities
    of recreational sites known as the travel cost method.
- This method measures the benefit (willingness to
    pay) from a recreational experience,-households’
    expenditures on the cost of travel to a desired
    recreational site.


- The basic idea behind this approach is that the
    services of a recreation site, cannot be adequately
    measured.
- Example- Gate price of Garden/Park is usually very
    low.
- Users of this Garden come from various locations.
- instead of the gate price, the price or the willingness
    to pay of each user can be approximated by her or his
    travel cost.
- method originated in the 1950 s, ever since used
    widely and to empirically estimate the demand
    (willingness to pay) for recreational sites.


Case Study: study by (Dixon and Hufschmidt 1986 **)**

**[recreational value of Lumpinee Park in Bangkok]**

1. Lumpinee a public park in the middle of Bangkok
2. Opportunity cost (the commercial value of the park for
    other activities) of maintaining park increasing
    steadily.
3. Doubt is about the economic viability of the park.
4. How did the recreational and amenity value of the
    park compared with its commercial value for other
    activities?


- Value of the park on the basis of the entrance fee
    (which was zero or nominal), - virtually nothing.
- Travel cost approach basically entails the construction
    of an empirical demand function for the public park.
- **Hypothesis-** the costs in money and time spent
    traveling to a free or nominally priced recreational site
    could be used to approximate consumers’ willingness
    to pay for the site.
- Other things remaining constant, inverse relationship
    between the travel cost and the number of visits to
    the given recreational site observed. In essence, this
    would represent the demand for a recreational site.


- The survey to obtain the basic needed data was conducted
    through interviews with 187 randomly selected visitors
    arriving from 17 differentadministrative districts.
- Afterwards, on the basis of this survey data and using
    statistical demand analysis, the demand for Lumpinee Park
    was estimated.
- Demand estimate, the consumers’ surplus enjoyed by
    visitors wasequivalent to $ 660 , 230 annually
- Large consumers’ surplus realized by its users ( not
    admission fee) clearly indicated that Lumpinee Park was a
    veryvaluableenvironmental asset.


- Two types of travel cost model: single-site and
    multiple-site models- omitting the prices and
    quantities of relevant substitutes will bias the
    resource valuations.
- The basic data needed to use travel cost methods
    include: the characteristics of individuals; the
    number of visits they make to the site; and
    information about their travel costs.
- more data are needed for multiple-site models.
- In addition to the characteristics of individual sites,
    data are needed to delineate the set of sites that is
    to be included in the model.
- to value changes in environmental quality, then site-
    quality data are needed.


##### Drawbacks:
