B Travel cost method

- Valuation of environmental services from recreational
    sites, such as national parks.
- A special technique to estimate the benefit from
    changes in the environmental amenities of
    recreational sites.
- Measures the benefit (willingness to pay) stemming
    from a recreational experience, (households’
    expenditures on the cost of travel).


- The basic idea - services of a recreation site, not
    adequately measured.
- Example- Gate price of camping ground, very low.
- Users of this campsite come from various locations.
- Instead of gate price, willingness to pay
    approximated by travel cost.
- Method originated in 1950 s, used widely for
    recreational sites.

## The contingent valuation method

- Common features of earlier approaches:
    - willingness to pay by using market prices either
       explicitly or implicitly.
    - stress exclusively on estimating use values, benefits
       or satisfactions of individuals directly utilizing the
       services (amenities) of natural environment.


- Several attributes of natural environment

## results satisfaction of individuals.

- But nonuse values beyond its recreational

## values to current users to extent people

## willing to pay to preserve it for future

## uses.


- Anthropocentric approaches not capture such

### nonuse value.

- Serious problem when resources involve long-

### time horizons, considerable uncertainty and/or

### irreversibility (Krutilla 1967 ; Arrow and Fisher

### 1974 )- characteristics common to many

### environmental assets.

- Contingent valuation general techniques to elicit

### willingness to pay in broad and inclusive sense

### Use and non use value.


### Principal components of nonuse values

- Nonuse values having three separable

### components, namely option, bequest and

### existence values or demands.


- **Option value** refers to insurance premium to retain the
    option of possible future use.
       - people willing to pay money for the preservation of
          wilderness or the protection of a unique site – not because
          current use but because willingness to reserve an option for
          futureaccess.
- People behave because of uncertainty regarding the
    future demand for or supply of these natural resources
    (Krutilla 1967 ;Johansson 1990 ).
- Consideration of option value important in uncertainty
    (Johansson 1990 ).


- **Bequest value** satisfaction people gain to preserve
    natural resource endowment for future generations.
       - Intergenerationalcomponentof theoptionvalue.
- Relevant, for unique resources, irreversible damage,
    and uncertainty regarding future generations’ demand
    for and/or supply.
- Examples- national parks, wilderness, tropical forests,
    aquifers, blue whales, coastal wetlands, coral reefs.
- Willingness to pay for preserving natural resources for
    the use of future generations.


- **Existence value** – people’s satisfaction from
    preservation of natural resources to sustaina habitat for
    fish, plants, wildlife, and so on.
- Willingness to pay (demand) for preserving the
    ecological integrity of the natural environment, i.e. for
    stewardship.
- Examples- debt-for-nature swaps by several
    internationally renowned conservation organizations
    for protecting the tropical forest.


**The conceptual model – Contingent Valuation method**

- Total value composed of several kinds of willingness to
    pay

(a) Total value = Use value + Nonuse value and

(b) Nonuse value = Option value + Bequest value +
Existence value.

- In case of uniqueness, irreversibility and uncertainty
    economic value should include both use and nonuse
    values.


- Valuation based on use value- allocation of an

- Real challenge, to capture willingness to pay for option,

    not reveal ‘true’ willingness to pay eg.-considering

    monetary value.
2. Information bias: quantity and quality of the
    information provided. information bias with
    regard to ‘existence value’ more pronounced.
3. Hypothetical bias: no ‘real’ transactions.
4. Difficulties with the reference group for pricing:


### Use of the contingent valuation approach in a

### real-world situation:

- Walsh et al. ( 1984 ) estimated the preservation value
    of incremental increases in wilderness designations in
    Colorado.
- Mail survey of 218 Colorado households.
- Participants shown four maps with different acreage
    designated as wilderness. 1. 2 , 2. 6 , 5 and 10 million
    acres respectively.
- information provided - scientific, historical and
    economic significance of wilderness areas for the
    current and future citizens of Colorado.


- Asked to indicate the maximum amount willing to pay
    annually.
- To report willingness to pay among the four categories
    of value: recreational use; option; existence; and
    bequest demands.
- Option, existence and bequest values -preservation
    value of wilderness. ( or total willingness to pay after
    minus recreational use benefits).
- Estimation of option, existence and bequest values
    independently.



**Inferences**

- Increasing area from 1. 2 to 2. 6 million acres increased
    total value by 46 % ($ 28. 5 to $ 41. 6 million).
       - doublingareas not doublevalue.
- Nonuse or preservation values represented a
    significant share for all four cases.
- Even at lower end ( 10 million acres), nonuse value 37 %
    of total value - significance of valuation techniques.
- Failure to account such benefits lead to decisions
    causing irreversible damage to wilderness areas and
    other environmental resources of a similar nature.

### Critical assessment of the economic

### approach to environmental valuation

- Four point criticisms.
- **First** , environmental values reducible to one-
    dimensional standard expressed in monetary terms.
- **Second** , Uncertainty make the very concept
    meaningless.
- **Third,** survey techniques confuse preferences with
    beliefs.
- **Fourth** , important ecological connections missed in
    individual component valuation.
